🌊 Wave Motion and Sound Waves (Part 1)

Types of waves, wave speed, energy in waves, and the wave equation

Waves Wave Speed Energy 45 min

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Understand the nature and types of waves (mechanical, electromagnetic, matter).
  • Distinguish between transverse and longitudinal waves.
  • Apply the wave equation and understand wave parameters.
  • Calculate the speed of a wave on a stretched string.
  • Understand the energy and power transmitted by a wave.
  • Apply the linear wave equation.
1

Types of Waves

Waves transfer energy without transferring matter. Mechanical waves require a medium. Electromagnetic waves do not. Matter waves are associated with particles. Examples include sound, light, water, and seismic waves.

Mechanical Waves: Require a medium to travel through. Examples: water waves, sound waves, seismic waves.

Electromagnetic Waves: Do not require a medium. Can travel through vacuum. Examples: light, radio waves, X-rays. All travel at c = 3.00 × 10⁸ m/s in vacuum.

Matter Waves: Associated with moving particles (electrons, protons, atoms). Predicted by quantum mechanics.

Mechanical waves transfer energy through a medium by causing particles to oscillate. The medium itself does not travel with the wave—only the disturbance propagates. Electromagnetic waves are self-propagating oscillations of electric and magnetic fields. They can travel through a vacuum, which is why we can see the Sun. Matter waves are a quantum mechanical concept—they describe the wave-like behavior of particles.

2

Transverse and Longitudinal Waves

In transverse waves, particles move perpendicular to the wave direction. In longitudinal waves, particles move parallel to the direction of propagation. Sound waves are longitudinal; light waves are transverse.

Transverse Waves: The particles of the medium move perpendicular to the direction of wave propagation. Example: waves on a string, electromagnetic waves.

Longitudinal Waves: The particles of the medium move parallel to the direction of wave propagation. Example: sound waves, seismic P-waves. The wave consists of compressions (high density) and rarefactions (low density).

Transverse waves can only propagate in solids (or on surfaces of liquids), because they require shear forces. Longitudinal waves can propagate in solids, liquids, and gases, because they involve compression and expansion of the medium. Sound waves are longitudinal because they propagate through air by compressing and expanding the air molecules.

3

Wave Parameters (Wavelength, Frequency, Period, Speed)

The speed of a wave is related to its wavelength and frequency by v = fλ = ω/k. The period is T = 1/f, and the angular frequency is ω = 2πf. The wave number is k = 2π/λ.

A sinusoidal wave is described by:

Wave Function y(x,t) = A sin(kx - ωt)

where A is the amplitude, k = 2π/λ is the wave number (rad/m), ω = 2πf = 2π/T is the angular frequency (rad/s), λ is the wavelength (m), f is the frequency (Hz), T is the period (s), and v = fλ = ω/k is the wave speed (m/s).

The wave equation y(x,t) = A sin(kx - ωt) describes a wave traveling to the right (in the positive x direction). For a wave traveling to the left, replace kx - ωt with kx + ωt. The parameters k and ω are related to the wavelength and period, respectively. The speed v = ω/k = fλ is the speed at which the wave profile moves.

4

Speed of a Wave on a Stretched String

The speed of a wave on a stretched string is v = √(T/μ), where T is the tension in the string and μ = m/L is the linear mass density. The speed depends on the properties of the string, not on the wave itself.

For a wave on a stretched string under tension T:

Wave Speed on String v = √(T/μ)

where T is the tension in the string (N), μ = m/L is the linear mass density (kg/m), m is the mass of the string, and L is the length of the string. Higher tension gives higher speed. Heavier strings (larger μ) give lower speed.

The formula v = √(T/μ) is derived from Newton's second law applied to a small element of the string. It shows that the wave speed depends only on the properties of the medium (the string), not on the wave itself. This is a general property of waves—the speed is determined by the medium, not by the source. For sound waves, the speed depends on the properties of the gas (temperature, pressure, etc.).

5

Energy and Power in a Wave

The power transmitted by a sinusoidal wave on a stretched string is P = ½μvω²A². The power is proportional to the square of the amplitude and the square of the angular frequency.

A wave transfers energy as it propagates. The average power transmitted by a sinusoidal wave on a stretched string is:

Wave Power P = ½μvω²A²

where μ is the linear mass density, v is the wave speed, ω is the angular frequency, and A is the amplitude.

The power depends on the properties of the string (μ and v) and the wave parameters (ω and A). The energy per unit length of the string is E/L = ½μω²A². The power is the energy per unit length times the wave speed. The power is proportional to A² and ω², which means that doubling the amplitude quadruples the power.

6

The Linear Wave Equation

The linear wave equation is ∂²y/∂x² = (1/v²) ∂²y/∂t². Any function of the form y = f(x ± vt) satisfies this equation, and the wave speed is v.

The linear wave equation is:

Wave Equation ∂²y/∂x² = (1/v²) ∂²y/∂t²

where y(x,t) is the wave function. This equation is satisfied by any wave function of the form y = f(x ± vt). The wave speed is v. For a wave on a string, this equation is derived from Newton's second law applied to a small element of the string.

The linear wave equation is one of the most important equations in physics. It describes how waves propagate through a medium. The equation is linear, which means that the superposition principle applies—the sum of any two solutions is also a solution. This is why waves can interfere with each other. The wave equation is the basis for understanding all wave phenomena, from sound to light to quantum mechanics.

🎓 Professor's Narrative

Wave motion is one of the most fundamental phenomena in physics. Waves transfer energy without transferring matter. They are everywhere—from the sound of a voice to the light from the Sun.

The key distinction is between transverse waves (where particles move perpendicular to the wave direction) and longitudinal waves (where particles move parallel to the wave direction). Sound waves are longitudinal, while light waves are transverse.

The speed of a wave is determined by the properties of the medium, not by the source. For a stretched string, the speed depends on the tension and the mass density. For sound waves, the speed depends on the temperature and pressure of the gas.

The wave equation is the mathematical description of wave motion. It's a second-order partial differential equation that describes how waves propagate. Understanding the wave equation is essential for understanding all wave phenomena.

❓ Ask the Professor

In a wave, the particles of the medium oscillate about their equilibrium positions, but they don't travel with the wave. Only the disturbance (energy) propagates. Think of a wave in a stadium—people stand up and sit down, but the "wave" travels around the stadium. The people (matter) don't move around the stadium, only the disturbance (energy) does.

Sound waves are longitudinal pressure waves. They require particles to collide and transfer pressure variations. In a vacuum, there are no particles to collide, so sound cannot travel. This is why you can't hear anything in space.

The speed of a wave is determined by the properties of the medium, not by the source. For a wave on a string, the speed depends on tension and mass density. For sound in air, the speed depends on temperature. For light, the speed depends on the refractive index of the medium (it's constant in vacuum).
