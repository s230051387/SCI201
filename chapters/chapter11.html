🔊 Wave Motion and Sound Waves (Part 2)

Sound waves, intensity, Doppler effect, and speed of sound

Sound Waves Intensity Doppler Effect 45 min

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Understand the properties of sound waves.
  • Define and calculate sound intensity and sound level (decibels).
  • Apply the Doppler effect to moving sources and observers.
  • Understand the speed of sound in various media.
1

Characteristics of Sound Waves

Sound waves are longitudinal mechanical waves that travel through any material medium. They consist of compressions (high pressure) and rarefactions (low pressure). The audible range for humans is 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz.

Sound waves are longitudinal waves—the particles of the medium oscillate parallel to the direction of propagation. They consist of:

Compressions: Regions of high pressure and density.

Rarefactions: Regions of low pressure and density.

Frequency ranges: Infrasonic (below 20 Hz), Audible (20 Hz to 20,000 Hz), Ultrasonic (above 20,000 Hz).

The speed of sound depends on the medium: Gases slowest (≈343 m/s in air at 20°C), Liquids faster (≈1480 m/s in water), Solids fastest (≈5000 m/s in steel). The speed of sound in air depends on temperature: v = 331 m/s √(1 + T/273), where T is in °C.

2

Displacement and Pressure in Sound Waves

A sound wave can be described in terms of displacement or pressure. ΔPmax = B k smax. Displacement and pressure are 90° out of phase.

A sinusoidal sound wave is described by:

Pressure Wave ΔP = ΔPmax sin(kx - ωt)

The displacement of the medium is:

Displacement s = smax cos(kx - ωt)

Pressure is maximum where displacement is zero, and vice versa. ΔPmax = B k smax = ρvω smax.

The pressure is maximum at the compressions and rarefactions, where the displacement of the medium is zero. The displacement is maximum where the pressure equals the equilibrium pressure. This phase relationship is a fundamental property of longitudinal waves.

3

Speed of Sound in Various Media

The speed of sound depends on the medium's density and compressibility. For a gas, v = √(B/ρ). For air, v = (331 m/s)√(1 + T/273), where T is in °C.

The speed of all mechanical waves follows: v = √(elastic property / inertial property). For sound waves:

Gas or liquid: v = √(B/ρ), where B is the bulk modulus.

Solid rod: v = √(Y/ρ), where Y is Young's modulus.

In air at 0°C, v = 331 m/s. At 20°C, v ≈ 343 m/s.

The speed of sound in a gas depends on temperature because the bulk modulus is proportional to pressure, which is related to temperature. At higher temperatures, molecules move faster and collide more frequently, so the speed of sound increases. The speed does not depend on pressure (for fixed temperature), because pressure increases both density and bulk modulus in the same proportion.

4

Sound Intensity and Sound Level

Sound intensity (I) is power per unit area: I = P/A. Sound level in decibels (dB): β = 10 log(I/I₀), where I₀ = 10⁻¹² W/m² is the threshold of hearing.

Sound intensity is the average power per unit area perpendicular to the direction of propagation:

Intensity I = P/A = ½ρvω²smax²

For spherical waves: I = P/(4πr²), so intensity decreases as 1/r².

The sound level in decibels:

Sound Level β = 10 log(I/I₀)

where I₀ = 10⁻¹² W/m². Doubling intensity increases level by 3 dB. A ten-fold increase in intensity corresponds to a 10 dB increase.

The decibel scale is logarithmic because the human ear responds logarithmically. A sound of 60 dB is not twice as loud as 30 dB—it is 10³ = 1000 times more intense. The threshold of pain is about 120 dB (I = 1 W/m²). Prolonged exposure above 85 dB can cause hearing damage.

5

The Doppler Effect

The Doppler effect is the change in observed frequency due to relative motion between source and observer. f' = f (v ± vD)/(v ± vS).

The observed frequency f' is:

Doppler Effect f' = f (v ± vD)/(v ± vS)

where v is the speed of sound, vD is the speed of the detector (observer), and vS is the speed of the source.

Sign conventions: Detector toward source: vD positive. Source toward detector: vS negative.

The Doppler effect is observed in many contexts: Sound (passing siren changes pitch), Light (redshift of distant galaxies), Radar (police radar guns), Medicine (Doppler ultrasound for blood flow). It results from relative motion changing the number of wave fronts per unit time.

6

Doppler Effect Cases

Source toward stationary observer: f' = f v/(v - vS) (higher). Source away: f' = f v/(v + vS) (lower). Observer toward source: f' = f (v + vD)/v (higher).

Source moving toward observer: f' = f v/(v - vS) → frequency increases.

Source moving away from observer: f' = f v/(v + vS) → frequency decreases.

Observer moving toward source: f' = f (v + vD)/v → frequency increases.

Observer moving away from source: f' = f (v - vD)/v → frequency decreases.

Both moving: Use general formula f' = f (v ± vD)/(v ± vS).

The Doppler effect is a powerful tool for measuring velocities. In astronomy, redshift of light from galaxies tells us how fast they're moving away. In medicine, Doppler ultrasound measures blood flow velocity. In police work, radar guns use the Doppler effect to measure car speeds. The effect is symmetric—it depends only on relative motion.

🎓 Professor's Narrative

Sound waves are longitudinal pressure waves that travel through any material medium. They are everywhere—from the sound of a voice to the roar of a jet engine.

The speed of sound depends on the properties of the medium. In air, the speed increases with temperature because the molecules move faster. In solids, sound travels faster because the molecules are more tightly coupled.

Sound intensity is measured in decibels, which is a logarithmic scale. The human ear can detect sounds from the threshold of hearing (0 dB) to the threshold of pain (120 dB). The decibel scale is logarithmic because the ear responds logarithmically to intensity.

The Doppler effect is the change in frequency due to relative motion between source and observer. It's a universal phenomenon—it applies to all types of waves, including sound and light. The effect is used in many applications, from astronomy to medicine to everyday life.

❓ Ask the Professor

As the siren approaches, sound waves are compressed, giving a higher frequency (pitch). As it passes and moves away, sound waves are stretched, giving a lower frequency. This is the classic example of the Doppler effect.

Sound intensity decreases as 1/r² for spherical waves. As you move away from the source, the same power is spread over a larger spherical surface area, so the intensity per unit area decreases.

The human ear is sensitive to frequencies from 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz. Ultrasonic sounds are above 20,000 Hz, so our ears can't detect them. However, some animals (like bats and dolphins) can hear ultrasonic frequencies and use them for echolocation.
