🧲 Magnetic Field

Magnetic poles, force on moving charges, current-carrying conductors, and right-hand rules

Magnetic Field Lorentz Force Right-Hand Rule 45 min

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Understand the nature of magnetic fields and magnetic poles.
  • Describe magnetic field lines and their properties.
  • Calculate the magnetic force on a moving charged particle.
  • Use right-hand rules to determine the direction of magnetic forces.
  • Calculate the radius and period of circular motion in a magnetic field.
  • Understand the magnetic force on a current-carrying conductor.
1

Magnetic Poles and Magnetic Fields

Magnets have north and south poles. Like poles repel, opposite poles attract. A magnetic field (B) surrounds any magnet and exerts forces on other magnets or moving charges. Magnetic field lines point from north to south outside the magnet.

A magnetic field surrounds a magnet and exerts forces on other magnets or moving charges. The direction of the magnetic field at any location is the direction a compass needle points (the north-seeking pole). Magnetic field lines outside a magnet point from the north pole to the south pole.

Unlike electric charges, magnetic poles always come in pairs—there are no magnetic monopoles. If you break a bar magnet in half, you get two smaller magnets, each with its own north and south poles. The Earth itself behaves like a giant bar magnet, with its south magnetic pole near the geographic north pole.

2

Earth's Magnetic Field

Earth behaves like a giant bar magnet. The geographic north pole is actually near Earth's south magnetic pole. The field is generated by convection currents in Earth's liquid iron core, and it reverses periodically.

The Earth's magnetic field is very similar to that of a bar magnet buried deep in the Earth. The north-seeking pole of a compass points toward the geographic north pole, which corresponds to the Earth's south magnetic pole. The source of the field is likely convection currents in the Earth's liquid outer core.

The Earth's magnetic field protects us from solar wind and cosmic radiation. The field reverses periodically, with the last reversal occurring about 780,000 years ago. These reversals are recorded in rocks as they cool, providing a history of the field's behavior over millions of years.

3

Magnetic Force on a Moving Charge

The magnetic force on a moving charge is F = qv × B. Magnitude: F = |q|vB sin θ, where θ is the angle between v and B. The force is perpendicular to both v and B.

A charged particle moving through a magnetic field experiences a magnetic force:

Magnetic Force F = qv × B

The magnitude is F = |q|vB sin θ. The force is zero when the particle moves parallel to the field (θ = 0° or 180°) and maximum when perpendicular (θ = 90°).

The magnetic force does no work on a charged particle because it is always perpendicular to the particle's displacement. It can change direction but not speed or kinetic energy. The SI unit of magnetic field is the tesla (T): 1 T = 1 N/(A·m). Another common unit is the gauss (G): 1 T = 10⁴ G.

4

Right-Hand Rules for Magnetic Force

Point fingers in direction of v, curl toward B, thumb points in direction of F for a positive charge. For a negative charge, the force is opposite.

Right-Hand Rule #1: Point fingers in direction of v, curl toward B, thumb points in direction of F (for positive charge).

Right-Hand Rule #2 (palm rule): Fingers in direction of B, thumb in direction of v, palm pushes in direction of F for positive charge.

The magnetic force is inherently three-dimensional. Mastering the right-hand rules is essential for magnetism. Remember: the magnetic force is always perpendicular to the plane containing v and B. For a negative charge (like an electron), reverse the direction given by the right-hand rule.

5

Motion of a Charged Particle in a Magnetic Field

When a charged particle moves perpendicular to a uniform B field, it undergoes circular motion. r = mv/(qB), T = 2πm/(qB), ω = qB/m.

When a charged particle moves perpendicular to a uniform magnetic field, the magnetic force provides the centripetal force:

Centripetal Force qvB = mv²/r

Solving for the radius:

Radius r = mv/(qB)

The period of the motion:

Period T = 2πr/v = 2πm/(qB)

The period is independent of speed—all particles with the same q/m ratio have the same period regardless of speed. This is the principle behind cyclotrons. The angular frequency ω = v/r = qB/m is called the cyclotron frequency.

6

Magnetic Force on a Current-Carrying Conductor

A magnetic field exerts a force on a current-carrying wire: F = IL × B. Magnitude: F = ILB sin θ. Direction given by the right-hand rule.

Since current consists of moving charges, a magnetic field exerts a force on a current-carrying conductor. For a straight wire of length L carrying current I:

Force on Wire F = IL × B

Magnitude: F = ILB sin θ, where θ is the angle between the wire and the magnetic field.

This force is used in electric motors, galvanometers, and loudspeakers. In a motor, current flows through a loop in a magnetic field, producing a torque that makes the loop rotate. For a wire of arbitrary shape, integrate: dF = I dL × B along the wire.

7

Differences Between Electric and Magnetic Forces

Electric forces act on charges regardless of motion, parallel to the field, and can do work. Magnetic forces act only on moving charges, perpendicular to the field, and cannot do work.

Property Electric Force Magnetic Force
Acts on charge Regardless of motion Only when moving
Direction Parallel or antiparallel to E Perpendicular to both v and B
Does work? Yes No
Changes KE? Yes No (only direction)
Depends on speed? No Yes

The fact that magnetic forces do no work is fundamental. The magnetic field cannot transfer energy to a charged particle; it can only redirect it. In cyclotrons and mass spectrometers, magnetic fields guide particles while electric fields accelerate them and change their energy.

8

Representations of Magnetic Fields (Dots and Crosses)

Dots (•) represent field coming out of the page. Crosses (×) represent field going into the page. Like the tip (dot) or tail (cross) of an arrow.

In diagrams, magnetic fields perpendicular to the page are represented by:

Dots (•): Field lines coming out of the page (toward you)

Crosses (×): Field lines going into the page (away from you)

This notation is essential for 3D problems. A charged particle moving in a plane with B perpendicular to the page follows a circular path. The force direction depends on the charge, velocity, and whether the field is coming out (•) or going in (×).

🎓 Professor's Narrative

Magnetism is fundamentally a relativistic effect—it arises from the motion of charges. The magnetic field is a vector field that exerts forces on moving charges. The key to understanding magnetism is mastering the right-hand rules and remembering that the magnetic force is always perpendicular to both the velocity of the particle and the magnetic field.

An important conceptual point is that magnetic forces cannot do work. This is because the force is always perpendicular to the displacement of the particle. The magnetic field can change the direction of a particle's motion but not its speed. This is why magnetic fields are used in particle accelerators to bend the paths of particles while electric fields are used to accelerate them.

The circular motion of charged particles in magnetic fields is a beautiful demonstration of the relationship between force and motion. The fact that the period of motion is independent of speed is a surprising and useful result that has led to practical applications like the cyclotron.

Finally, remember that while magnetic poles are analogous to electric charges in some ways, there are no magnetic monopoles. Every magnet has both a north and a south pole, and you can't isolate one pole from the other.

Work is defined as W = F · d, and since the force is perpendicular to the displacement, the dot product is zero. The magnetic field changes only the direction of the particle's motion, not its speed or kinetic energy.

The tesla is the SI unit of magnetic field: 1 T = 1 N/(A·m). The gauss is a non-SI unit: 1 T = 10⁴ G. Earth's magnetic field is about 0.5 × 10⁻⁴ T (0.5 gauss).

According to Maxwell's equations, magnetic field lines are always closed loops (they have no beginning or end). This means magnetic poles always come in pairs. While some theories predict the existence of magnetic monopoles, they have never been observed in nature.
