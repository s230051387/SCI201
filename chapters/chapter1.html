⚡ Electric Potential

Electric potential energy, potential difference, voltage, and the relationship between electric field and potential

Electric Potential Potential Energy Voltage 45 min

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Define electric potential energy and understand its relationship to work done by electric forces.
  • Understand electric potential as potential energy per unit charge.
  • Calculate potential difference between two points in an electric field.
  • Relate electric field to potential difference.
  • Apply conservation of energy to charged particles in electric fields.
1

Electric Potential Energy (U or PE)

When a charge q is placed in an electric field E, the electric force qE acts on the charge. The change in electric potential energy is the negative of the work done by this electric force.

Change in Electric Potential Energy ΔU = -W

Electrostatic force is conservative, which allows us to describe electrostatic phenomena in terms of electric potential energy. When a charge q is free to move in an electric field E, the electric field will do work on the charge, given as:

Work Done by Electric Force W = qEd

The change in electric potential energy of the charge-field system is the negative of the work done by the electric force:

Relationship Between Work and Potential Energy ΔU = -W

For a finite displacement of a charge from a high potential point (a) to a low potential point (b), the change of potential energy is:

Potential Energy Change Ub - Ua = -qEd

where d is the distance between the charges. For a potential energy to exist, there must be a system of two or more charges. A positive charge naturally tends to move from a high potential to a low potential.

2

Electric Potential (V)

Electric potential is potential energy per unit charge.

Electric Potential V = UE / q

Dividing the potential energy by the charge provides a physical quantity called the electric potential (or simply the potential), denoted as V. The formula is given as:

Definition of Electric Potential V = UE / q

Electric potential depends only on the source charge distribution and has a specific value at every point in an electric field. Because potential energy is a scalar quantity, electric potential is also a scalar quantity.

3

Potential Difference (Voltage)

Potential difference is the change in electric potential energy divided by the charge. The SI unit is the volt (V).

Potential Difference Vba = ΔUE / q

The potential difference ΔV = Vba = Vb - Va between two points in an electric field is defined as the change in electric potential energy of the system when a charge q is moved between the points, divided by the charge:

Potential Difference Definition Vba = (Ub - Ua) / q

The difference in potential energy is equal to the negative of the work Wba done by the electric field to move the charge from a to b. Therefore, the potential difference is:

Potential Difference in Terms of Work Vba = ΔV = -Wba / q

The SI unit of both electric potential and potential difference is joules per coulomb, which is defined as a volt (V): 1 V = 1 J/C.

4

Work Done in Terms of Electric Potential

The work done by an external agent moving a charge q through an electric field at a constant velocity is:

Work by External Agent W = qΔV

If an agent moves a charge from point a to point b without changing the kinetic energy of the charge, the agent performs work that changes the potential energy of the system.

This relationship is expressed as W = ΔUE. Consequently, the work done by an external agent in moving a charge q through an electric field at a constant velocity is calculated as:

Work Done by External Agent W = qΔV
5

Energy Conservation and Accelerated Charges

When a charge accelerates through a potential difference, its loss in potential energy becomes kinetic energy.

By the conservation of energy, the potential energy lost by a charged particle becomes kinetic energy (KE). The equation is:

Conservation of Energy ΔKE + ΔPE = 0 → ΔKE = -ΔPE

For an electron starting from rest, the initial kinetic energy is zero, so the final KE = -qVba. While electric potential energy depends only on charge and voltage (not mass), the final speed of the particle does depend on its mass. Additionally, negative electrons are attracted toward a positive electrode and repelled from a negative electrode.

6

Energy and the Direction of Electric Field

Electric field lines point toward decreasing potential. Moving a positive charge in the field's direction loses potential energy.

Electric field lines always point in the direction of decreasing electric potential. A system consisting of a positive charge and an electric field loses electric potential energy when the charge moves in the direction of the field.

The electric field does work on a positive charge when it moves in the field's direction, causing it to gain kinetic energy while the system's potential energy decreases by an equal amount. Conversely, a system with a negative charge gains potential energy when moving in the direction of the field. For a negative charge to move in the direction of the field, an external agent must do positive work on the charge.

7

Electric Field and Potential Difference

The electric field is related to potential difference by:

Electric Field from Potential E = -Vba / d

The relationship between a uniform electric field E and potential difference Vba is:

Uniform Field Relationship E = -Vba / d = -(Vb - Va) / d

where d is the distance between the points.

The negative sign in the equation indicates that the electric field is directed from a higher potential to a lower potential. Because potential difference has the unit of electric field times distance, the SI unit of the electric field (N/C) can be expressed as volts per meter: 1 N/C = 1 V/m.

8

Electric Potential vs. Gravitational Potential

There is an analogy between gravitational and electrical potential energy.

An analogy exists between gravitational and electrical potential energy; just as a more massive rock has more potential energy, so does a larger charge.

If a charge Q in an electric field is replaced by a charge 2Q, the electric potential stays the same, but the electric potential energy of the system doubles.

🎓 Professor's Narrative

The study of electricity requires a solid grasp of energy and work. Electrostatic force is conservative, which allows us to describe electrostatic phenomena in terms of electric potential energy. You must master the distinction between potential energy (U) and electric potential (V); electric potential is simply the potential energy divided by the charge (V = UE/q). Always remember that electric field lines point in the direction of decreasing electric potential, and a positive charge naturally tends to move from high potential to low potential.
