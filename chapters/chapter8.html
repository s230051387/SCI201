🔄 The Second Law of Thermodynamics

Heat engines, efficiency, Carnot cycle, and entropy

Second Law Heat Engines Carnot 45 min

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Understand the concept of irreversibility.
  • Explain heat engines and their operation.
  • Calculate the efficiency of a heat engine.
  • State the Kelvin-Planck and Clausius statements of the Second Law.
  • Understand reversible and irreversible processes.
  • Describe the Carnot cycle and Carnot efficiency.
1

Irreversibility and the Second Law

The Second Law of Thermodynamics asserts that processes occur in a certain direction and that energy has quality as well as quantity. All natural processes are irreversible—they occur spontaneously in one direction only.

The First Law states that energy is conserved but places no restriction on the direction of energy flow. The Second Law addresses this: processes occur in a certain direction and cannot be reversed. An irreversible process is one that occurs naturally in one direction only. For example, heat flows spontaneously from hot to cold, never from cold to hot. All natural processes are irreversible to some degree.

The Second Law has profound implications. It explains why we cannot build a perfect heat engine that converts all heat into work. It explains why time has a direction (the arrow of time). The Second Law is intimately connected to the concept of entropy—a measure of disorder. In any natural process, the total entropy of the universe increases. This is why we can't "unburn" fuel or "unbreak" an egg.

2

Heat Engines

A heat engine takes in energy by heat (Qh) from a hot reservoir, does work (Weng), and expels some energy as heat (Qc) to a cold reservoir. Weng = |Qh| - |Qc|.

A heat engine operates in a cyclic process:

1. Absorbs energy Qh from a high-temperature reservoir.

2. Does work Weng on its surroundings.

3. Expels energy Qc to a low-temperature reservoir.

Since it's a cyclic process, the change in internal energy is zero (ΔU = 0), so:

Net Work Weng = |Qh| - |Qc|

Heat engines are everywhere—from car engines to power plants to the human body. The Second Law tells us that no engine can be 100% efficient—some heat must always be exhausted to a cold reservoir. The cold reservoir is typically the environment (atmosphere, river, etc.).

3

Thermal Efficiency

Thermal efficiency (e) is the ratio of net work done to energy input: e = Weng/|Qh| = 1 - |Qc|/|Qh|. Efficiency is always less than 100%.

Thermal efficiency is defined as:

Efficiency e = Weng / |Qh|

Since Weng = |Qh| - |Qc|, this can be written as:

Efficiency e = 1 - |Qc| / |Qh|

Efficiency is a measure of how much of the input energy is converted into useful work. In practice, all heat engines have efficiencies less than 100%.

To achieve 100% efficiency, Qc would have to be zero, which is impossible according to the Second Law. The efficiency of a heat engine is limited by the temperatures of the reservoirs. Even an ideal Carnot engine has efficiency e = 1 - Tc/Th, which is less than 100% as long as Tc > 0 and Th is finite.

4

The Kelvin-Planck Statement

The Kelvin-Planck statement: It is impossible to construct a heat engine that, operating in a cycle, produces no effect other than the input of energy by heat from a reservoir and the performance of an equal amount of work.

The Kelvin-Planck statement says that no heat engine can be 100% efficient. Some energy Qc must always be expelled to the environment. It is impossible to convert all heat into work without any other effect. This means:

• Weng can never equal |Qh|

• Qc cannot be zero

• Efficiency e can never equal 100%

This statement has profound practical implications. Any engine that produces work must also exhaust some heat. This is why power plants need cooling towers—they must reject waste heat to the environment. The Kelvin-Planck statement is equivalent to the Clausius statement—if one were false, the other would also be false.

5

The Clausius Statement

The Clausius statement: It is impossible to construct a cyclical machine whose sole effect is to transfer energy continuously by heat from one object to another at a higher temperature without the input of energy by work.

The Clausius statement says that heat cannot flow spontaneously from a cold object to a hot object. To make heat flow from cold to hot, you must do work (as in a refrigerator or heat pump). This means:

• Energy does not spontaneously transfer by heat from a cold object to a hot object.

• A perfect refrigerator (one that cools without requiring work) is impossible.

The Clausius statement is equivalent to the Kelvin-Planck statement. If you could build a perfect refrigerator (heat flowing from cold to hot without work), you could use it with a heat engine to violate the Kelvin-Planck statement. The Second Law prevents both.

6

Reversible and Irreversible Processes

A reversible process is one where the system can be returned to its initial state along the same path. An irreversible process cannot be reversed. All natural processes are irreversible.

Reversible Process: A process in which every point along the path is an equilibrium state, and the system can be returned to its initial state along the same path. Reversible processes are an idealization; they require the process to occur infinitely slowly (quasi-static) with no dissipative effects.

Irreversible Process: A process that cannot be reversed. All natural processes are irreversible. Examples include heat flow from hot to cold, free expansion of a gas into a vacuum, and friction and turbulence.

Reversible processes are important because they represent the maximum possible efficiency. The Carnot engine is an ideal reversible engine. In reality, all processes have some irreversibility due to friction, turbulence, and other dissipative effects. The more quickly a process occurs, the more irreversible it tends to be.

7

The Carnot Cycle and Carnot Engine

The Carnot engine is a theoretical engine operating between two reservoirs in a cycle of two isothermal and two adiabatic processes. It is the most efficient engine possible: eC = 1 - Tc/Th.

The Carnot cycle consists of four steps:

A → B (Isothermal Expansion): Gas in contact with hot reservoir, absorbs heat Qh at temperature Th.

B → C (Adiabatic Expansion): No heat exchange, gas cools from Th to Tc.

C → D (Isothermal Compression): Gas in contact with cold reservoir, expels heat Qc at temperature Tc.

D → A (Adiabatic Compression): No heat exchange, gas warms from Tc to Th.

For a Carnot engine, Qc/Qh = Tc/Th, so:

Carnot Efficiency eC = 1 - Tc/Th

The Carnot efficiency is the maximum possible efficiency for any engine operating between two given temperatures. To achieve higher efficiency, increase Th or decrease Tc. In practice, Tc is often near room temperature (about 300 K), so the only way to improve efficiency is to increase Th. This is why power plants use superheated steam at high temperatures.

8

Carnot's Theorem

Carnot's theorem: No real heat engine operating between two reservoirs can be more efficient than a Carnot engine operating between the same two reservoirs. All real engines are less efficient because they are irreversible.

Carnot's theorem has two parts:

1. All Carnot engines operating between the same two temperatures have the same efficiency, regardless of the working substance.

2. No real (irreversible) engine operating between the same two temperatures can be more efficient than a Carnot engine.

The efficiency of a real engine is reduced by friction, energy losses through conduction, and other irreversible processes.

Carnot's theorem provides an upper bound on the efficiency of any heat engine. It tells us that the efficiency of an engine is limited by the temperatures of the reservoirs. The theorem also provides a way to define an absolute temperature scale—the Carnot efficiency is independent of the working substance, so it can be used to define temperature.

🎓 Professor's Narrative

The Second Law of Thermodynamics is perhaps the most profound law in physics. It tells us that not all energy is created equal—energy has quality as well as quantity. Heat at high temperature is more useful (has more "quality") than heat at low temperature.

Heat engines are devices that convert heat into work. They are essential to modern civilization—from car engines to power plants. But the Second Law tells us that no engine can be perfect. Some heat must always be rejected to a cold reservoir.

The Carnot engine is an idealization—a perfectly reversible engine—that sets the maximum possible efficiency. Real engines are less efficient due to friction, heat losses, and other irreversibilities. The Carnot efficiency e = 1 - Tc/Th is a fundamental limit that depends only on the temperatures of the reservoirs.

The Second Law also tells us that processes occur in a certain direction. Heat flows from hot to cold, not the other way around. This directionality is what gives time its arrow—the universe is always moving toward greater disorder (entropy).

❓ Ask the Professor

According to the Second Law, some heat must always be rejected to a cold reservoir. If we had a 100% efficient engine, we would be converting all heat into work with no other effect, which violates the Kelvin-Planck statement. This would be like a perpetual motion machine of the second kind.

A heat engine converts heat into work (it takes energy from hot and delivers work). A refrigerator uses work to move heat from cold to hot (it takes work to pump heat from a cold space to a hot environment). Both are governed by the Second Law.

The Carnot engine is a reversible engine—it operates through a cycle of equilibrium states with no friction or other dissipative losses. Any irreversibility (friction, turbulence, heat loss) reduces efficiency. Since the Carnot engine has no irreversibilities, it achieves the maximum possible efficiency.
