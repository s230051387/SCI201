⚡ Electric Potential Due to Continuous Charge Distribution

Point charges, superposition, equipotential surfaces, and electric dipoles

Electric Potential Superposition Dipole 45 min

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Calculate the electric potential due to a single point charge.
  • Apply the superposition principle to find the total electric potential due to multiple point charges.
  • Calculate the total potential energy of a system of multiple point charges.
  • Understand equipotential surfaces and their relationship to electric field lines.
  • Describe the electric potential of an electric dipole.
1

Electric Potential Due to Point Charges

The electric potential at a distance r from a single point charge q is given by V = kq/r, where V is taken to be zero at infinity.

Potential of a Point Charge V = kq / r

The electric potential due to a point charge q at a distance r can be derived from its electric field. The potential is conventionally taken to be zero at infinity, where the electric field is zero. The formula is:

Point Charge Potential V = k · q / r

where k = 8.99 × 10⁹ N·m²/C².

Using the definition of potential and integrating the electric field of a point charge from infinity to a point P at distance r yields the same result. The potential can be positive or negative, depending on the sign of the charge q. It is a scalar quantity, so you do not need to consider direction, only the sign of the charge.

2

Electric Potential for a Positive or Negative Charge

For a positive charge, the potential is positive and decreases as you move away. For a negative charge, the potential is negative and increases (becomes less negative) as you move away.

The formula V = kq/r is applied directly. Include the sign of the charge in your calculation. For example, a +20 μC charge at 0.50 m yields V = 3.6 × 10⁵ V. A -20 μC charge at the same distance yields V = -3.6 × 10⁵ V.

The sign of the potential is a direct result of the sign of the source charge. This is a crucial distinction from electric field, which always points away from a positive charge and toward a negative charge. The potential's sign reflects the work required to bring a positive test charge from infinity.

3

Electric Potential Due to Multiple Point Charges (Superposition)

The total electric potential at a point due to several point charges is the algebraic sum of the potentials due to each individual charge.

Superposition Principle V = ke Σ (qi / ri)

Because electric potential is a scalar, the superposition principle is applied by adding the scalar contributions, not by vector addition. For a group of point charges q₁, q₂, q₃..., the total potential at point P is:

Multiple Charges Potential V = ke (q₁/r₁ + q₂/r₂ + q₃/r₃ + ...)

This is a powerful simplification. Unlike the electric field, which requires vector addition, the potential is a scalar sum. This means finding the potential due to multiple charges is often much easier than finding the electric field. You simply calculate the potential from each charge (including its sign) and add the numbers together.

4

Potential Energy of a System of Charges

The total electric potential energy of a system of multiple charges is the sum of the potential energies of every pair of charges.

For a system of more than two charged particles, the total potential energy is calculated by summing the interaction energy for every pair. For three charges q₁, q₂, and q₃:

System Potential Energy UE = ke (q₁q₂/r₁₂ + q₁q₃/r₁₃ + q₂q₃/r₂₃)

This energy represents the work required to assemble the system of charges from infinity. Each term represents the potential energy of a pair of point charges. The signs of the charges are crucial: opposite charges give a negative potential energy (a bound, attractive system), while like charges give a positive potential energy (a repulsive, unbound system).

5

Relationship Between Electric Field and Potential

The electric field is the negative gradient of the electric potential. The field points in the direction of the steepest decrease in potential.

In a uniform field, the relationship is E = -ΔV/Δs (where Δs is the displacement). More generally, Ex = -∂V/∂x. If you know the potential as a function of position, you can find the electric field.

This is one of the most important relationships in electromagnetism. It ties the force concept (field) to the energy concept (potential). It also explains why equipotential surfaces are perpendicular to electric field lines: no work is done moving a charge along a surface of constant potential, meaning the force (and thus the field) has no component along the surface.

6

Equipotential Surfaces

An equipotential surface has the same potential everywhere. No work is required to move a charge on it, and it is always perpendicular to electric field lines.

Equipotential surfaces are the 3D extension of equipotential lines. For a uniform field, they are planes perpendicular to the field. For a point charge, they are concentric spheres. Field lines are always perpendicular to these surfaces.

The electric field is strongest where equipotential surfaces are closest together, because a large change in potential over a small distance implies a strong field. They are analogous to contour lines on a topographic map, where the steepest slope (the "field") is perpendicular to the contour lines.

7

Electric Dipole Potential

The electric potential of a dipole falls off as 1/r². It depends on the dipole moment p = ql and the angle.

For a point P far from the dipole (r >> l), the potential is:

Dipole Potential V ≈ k (p cos θ) / r²

where p = ql is the dipole moment, l is the separation, and θ is the angle from the dipole axis.

The dipole potential falls off much faster (as 1/r²) than a single charge (1/r). This is because the two charges' potentials nearly cancel at large distances. The dipole moment is a vector pointing from negative to positive charge, crucial for understanding polar molecules and dielectrics.

8

Calculating Field from Potential (Visualization)

By examining the spacing of equipotential surfaces, you can determine the strength and direction of the electric field. Field is strongest where equipotentials are closest.

Field lines are perpendicular to equipotential lines and point toward decreasing potential. The magnitude of the field is larger where equipotential lines are closer together.

This applies E = -dV/ds practically. If equipotential lines are close together, distance ds is small, resulting in a large electric field. This skill is essential for interpreting diagrams and connecting abstract potential to measurable force.

🎓 Professor's Narrative

The superposition principle is your most powerful tool when dealing with multiple charges. Since potential is a scalar, you can simply add the contributions from each source charge, which is much simpler than vector addition for electric fields. Remember, the sign of the charge determines the sign of its potential contribution.

Pay close attention to the distinction between potential energy of a system (which depends on a charge placed in the field) and the electric potential at a point (which is a property of the field itself). The potential is like the altitude on a topographic map; it exists everywhere, independent of whether you put a ball on the hill. The potential energy is the energy that ball would have.

Finally, always remember the fundamental relationship: E = -ΔV/Δs. This is the "master equation" connecting the force-based field and the energy-based potential. It's the key to understanding why equipotential surfaces and electric field lines are always perpendicular.
