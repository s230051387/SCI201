💡 The Nature of Light, Reflection and Refraction (Part 2)

Refraction, Snell's law, total internal reflection, and optical fibers

Refraction Snell's Law Optical Fibers 45 min

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Understand the concept of total internal reflection.
  • Calculate the critical angle.
  • Explain applications of total internal reflection (optical fibers).
1

Total Internal Reflection

Total internal reflection occurs when light traveling in a medium with higher index of refraction strikes the boundary with a lower index medium at an angle greater than the critical angle. All the light is reflected; none is transmitted.

When light travels from a medium with higher index n₁ to a medium with lower index n₂ (n₁ > n₂), it bends away from the normal. As the angle of incidence θ₁ increases, the angle of refraction θ₂ approaches 90°. The critical angle is the angle of incidence for which the refracted ray is at 90° to the normal. For angles greater than the critical angle, total internal reflection occurs.

The critical angle is: sin θc = n₂/n₁ or θc = sin⁻¹(n₂/n₁).

Total internal reflection occurs only when light travels from a higher index to a lower index medium. The reflected light is 100%—there is no transmission. This is the principle behind optical fibers, where light is guided through a fiber by repeated total internal reflection. It's also why diamonds sparkle—light that enters the diamond undergoes total internal reflection many times before it exits.

2

The Critical Angle

The critical angle is the angle of incidence at which the refracted ray is at 90° to the normal. sin θc = n₂/n₁. For angles greater than θc, total internal reflection occurs.

At the critical angle, the angle of refraction is 90°:

Critical Angle n₁ sin θc = n₂ sin 90° = n₂
Critical Angle Formula sin θc = n₂/n₁

Example: For an air-water interface (nwater = 1.33, nair = 1.00): sin θc = 1.00/1.33 = 0.752, θc = 48.8°.

The critical angle depends on the indices of refraction of the two media. For a higher index difference, the critical angle is smaller. For example, diamond (n = 2.42) has a critical angle of about 24.4° with air, which is why diamonds are so brilliant—light that enters the diamond is trapped by total internal reflection and can't easily escape.

3

Optical Fibers

Optical fibers use total internal reflection to guide light along their length. Light enters the fiber, strikes the sides at angles greater than the critical angle, and is reflected internally. The fiber can be bent around corners.

An optical fiber consists of a core of higher index glass surrounded by a cladding of lower index glass. Light entering the core at a suitable angle undergoes total internal reflection at the core-cladding boundary and is guided along the fiber. The fiber can be bent because the total internal reflection continues as long as the angle of incidence exceeds the critical angle.

Optical fibers are used extensively in telecommunications because they can carry much more information than copper wires. They are also used in medical imaging (endoscopy). The 2009 Nobel Prize in Physics was awarded to Charles K. Kao for his discovery of how to transmit light signals over long distances through thin glass fibers. The fibers are made of very pure glass to minimize absorption losses.

⚖️ Total Internal Reflection vs Regular Reflection

Property Regular Reflection Total Internal Reflection
Condition Any angle of incidence θ₁ > θc and n₁ > n₂
Reflected Intensity Partial (some transmitted) 100% (all reflected)
Transmitted Light Yes (some passes through) No (none passes through)
Medium Requirement Any interface Higher to lower index only

🎓 Professor's Narrative

Total internal reflection is a fascinating phenomenon that occurs when light tries to go from a higher index to a lower index medium. It's the reason why diamonds sparkle and why optical fibers can guide light around corners.

The critical angle is the key concept—it's the angle at which the refracted ray skims along the surface. If the angle of incidence is greater than the critical angle, the light is completely reflected. No light is transmitted.

Optical fibers are one of the most important applications of total internal reflection. They are the backbone of modern telecommunications, carrying vast amounts of information over long distances. The fibers are made of very pure glass to minimize absorption, and the light is guided by total internal reflection.

❓ Ask the Professor

Total internal reflection requires the refracted ray to be at 90° or more. This can only happen when the ray is bent away from the normal, which occurs when light goes from a higher index to a lower index (n₁ > n₂). If light goes from lower to higher (n₁ < n₂), it bends toward the normal and can never reach 90°.

An optical fiber consists of a core with a higher index of refraction surrounded by a cladding with a lower index. Light entering the core at a small enough angle undergoes total internal reflection at the core-cladding boundary. This reflection continues as the light travels along the fiber, even when the fiber is bent.
