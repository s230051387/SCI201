🔄 Oscillatory Motion and Simple Pendulum

Simple harmonic motion, spring-mass systems, pendulums, and energy in SHM

SHM Pendulum Hooke's Law 45 min

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Understand simple harmonic motion (SHM) and its characteristics.
  • Define amplitude, period, frequency, and angular frequency.
  • Apply Hooke's law to spring-mass systems.
  • Derive equations for displacement, velocity, and acceleration in SHM.
  • Understand the energy of an SHM oscillator.
  • Apply SHM concepts to the simple pendulum.
1

Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM)

Simple harmonic motion (SHM) occurs when the restoring force is proportional to the displacement from equilibrium and directed toward equilibrium: F = -kx. The displacement follows a sinusoidal function of time.

SHM is the motion of an object where the force acting on it is proportional to its displacement from equilibrium and always directed toward the equilibrium position. The motion is sinusoidal:

Displacement x(t) = A cos(ωt + φ)

where x(t) is the displacement at time t, A is the amplitude (maximum displacement), ω is the angular frequency (rad/s), and φ is the phase constant (determines initial conditions).

SHM is the simplest form of oscillatory motion and serves as a model for many physical systems, including pendulums, springs, and even molecular vibrations. The key characteristic is the linear restoring force (F = -kx), which leads to sinusoidal motion. The motion is periodic—it repeats itself after each period T = 2π/ω. The frequency f = 1/T is the number of oscillations per second.

2

The Spring-Mass System

For a mass attached to a spring, Hooke's law gives the restoring force: Fs = -kx. Angular frequency: ω = √(k/m), period: T = 2π√(m/k), frequency: f = 1/(2π)√(k/m).

A mass m attached to a spring of force constant k on a frictionless surface exhibits SHM. Hooke's law gives:

Hooke's Law Fs = -kx

Newton's second law gives: -kx = ma = m(d²x/dt²)

This differential equation has the solution x(t) = A cos(ωt + φ), where:

Angular Frequency ω = √(k/m)
Period T = 2π/ω = 2π√(m/k)

The period and frequency depend only on the mass and the spring constant, not on the amplitude. This is called isochronism—the period is independent of the amplitude. This property makes SHM oscillators useful for timekeeping. A stiffer spring (larger k) gives a higher frequency. A larger mass gives a lower frequency.

3

Displacement, Velocity, and Acceleration in SHM

The displacement, velocity, and acceleration in SHM are sinusoidal. Velocity leads displacement by 90° (π/2), and acceleration is 180° (π) out of phase with displacement.

The equations for SHM are:

Displacement x(t) = A cos(ωt + φ)
Velocity v(t) = -ωA sin(ωt + φ)
Acceleration a(t) = -ω²A cos(ωt + φ) = -ω²x(t)

The maximum values are: vmax = ωA, amax = ω²A = (k/m)A.

When displacement is maximum (x = A), velocity is zero and acceleration is maximum (negative). When displacement is zero (at equilibrium), velocity is maximum and acceleration is zero. Velocity leads displacement by 90°. Acceleration is always opposite to displacement (a = -ω²x).

4

Energy in SHM

Total mechanical energy in SHM is constant: E = K + U = ½kA². Kinetic energy is maximum at equilibrium, potential energy is maximum at the turning points.

For a spring-mass system:

Kinetic Energy K = ½mv² = ½kA² sin²(ωt + φ)
Potential Energy U = ½kx² = ½kA² cos²(ωt + φ)
Total Energy E = K + U = ½kA² = ½mω²A²

At any position x: v = ±ω√(A² - x²).

Energy conservation is a powerful tool for solving SHM problems. The total energy is constant, with kinetic energy converted to potential energy and back. At the turning points (x = ±A), all energy is potential (U = ½kA², K = 0). At equilibrium (x = 0), all energy is kinetic (K = ½kA², U = 0).

5

SHM and Circular Motion

SHM is the projection of uniform circular motion onto a diameter. The angular speed of the circular motion is the same as the angular frequency of the SHM.

If a particle moves in a circle of radius A with constant angular speed ω, the projection of its position onto the x-axis is:

Projection x(t) = A cos(ωt + φ)

This is exactly the same as the displacement in SHM. The projection of velocity is v(t) = -ωA sin(ωt + φ), and the projection of acceleration is a(t) = -ω²A cos(ωt + φ).

This analogy allows us to visualize SHM as the shadow of circular motion. The amplitude of the SHM is the radius of the circle. The angular frequency of the SHM is the angular speed of the circular motion. The phase constant is the initial angle of the particle on the circle.

6

The Simple Pendulum

A simple pendulum is a mass suspended by a light string. For small angles (θ < 10°), it exhibits SHM with ω = √(g/L) and T = 2π√(L/g). Period is independent of mass.

A simple pendulum consists of a bob of mass m attached to a string of length L. The restoring force is the tangential component of gravity:

Restoring Force Ft = -mg sin θ

For small angles, sin θ ≈ θ, so Ft = -mgθ. Using θ = x/L, the equation of motion becomes:

Equation of Motion d²θ/dt² = -(g/L)θ

This is the SHM equation with ω = √(g/L) and T = 2π√(L/g).

The period depends only on the length and g, not on the mass or amplitude (for small angles). This property makes pendulums useful for timekeeping—grandfather clocks use pendulums. The pendulum also provides a method for measuring g by measuring the period and length.

7

Period and Frequency of a Pendulum

Period: T = 2π√(L/g). Frequency: f = 1/(2π)√(g/L). These depend only on length and g, not on mass or amplitude (for small angles).

For a simple pendulum:

Period T = 2π√(L/g)
Frequency f = 1/T = 1/(2π)√(g/L)
Angular Frequency ω = 2πf = √(g/L)

Longer pendulums have longer periods. Stronger gravity gives shorter periods.

The independence of period from amplitude is called isochronism. It's what makes pendulums useful as timekeepers. For larger angles, the period depends on amplitude, which is why pendulum clocks use small amplitudes. The formula T = 2π√(L/g) is valid for angles less than about 10°.

8

Applications of SHM

SHM is a model for many physical systems: springs, pendulums, molecular vibrations, and electrical oscillators (LC circuits). It provides a foundation for understanding waves.

Examples of SHM in nature and technology:

Mechanical: Springs, pendulums, balance wheels in watches

Atomic: Molecular vibrations (infrared spectroscopy)

Electrical: LC circuits (radio transmitters and receivers)

Acoustical: Musical instruments (vibrating strings and air columns)

Seismic: Earthquake waves (oscillations of the ground)

SHM is the foundation for understanding wave motion. A wave is essentially a coupled system of oscillators, with energy transferred from one oscillator to the next. The mathematics of SHM (sinusoidal functions, complex exponentials) is used extensively in physics, engineering, and signal processing.

🎓 Professor's Narrative

Oscillatory motion is everywhere in nature—from the swinging of a pendulum to the vibrations of atoms in a molecule. Simple harmonic motion is the simplest form of oscillatory motion, and it provides the foundation for understanding more complex oscillatory systems.

The key to SHM is the linear restoring force: F = -kx. This force leads to sinusoidal motion with a period that is independent of the amplitude. This property, called isochronism, is why pendulums and springs are used as timekeepers.

Energy conservation is a powerful tool for analyzing SHM. The total energy is constant, with kinetic energy converting to potential energy and back. At any point in the motion, the sum of kinetic and potential energies equals ½kA².

The simple pendulum is a classic example of SHM for small angles. Its period depends only on the length of the string and the acceleration due to gravity, not on the mass of the bob. This makes pendulums useful for measuring g and for keeping time.

Finally, remember that SHM is the basis for understanding waves. A wave is a disturbance that propagates through a medium, with each point in the medium undergoing SHM. The mathematics of SHM is essential for understanding everything from sound waves to light waves.

❓ Ask the Professor

The restoring force F = -kx is linear—it increases linearly with displacement. This leads to a differential equation (d²x/dt² = -(k/m)x) whose solution is sinusoidal with angular frequency ω = √(k/m), which is independent of amplitude. The amplitude only affects the starting point.

The restoring force is the tangential component of gravity: Ft = -mg sin θ. The acceleration is at = Ft/m = -g sin θ. Notice that the mass cancels out—the acceleration depends only on g and θ. Therefore, the period is independent of mass.

The approximation sin θ ≈ θ is no longer valid. The period becomes dependent on amplitude—larger amplitudes have longer periods. This is why pendulum clocks use small amplitudes (typically less than 5°). For larger angles, the period is given by an elliptic integral.
