🌡️ Temperature and Thermodynamics

Temperature scales, thermal expansion, and thermal equilibrium

Temperature Thermal Expansion Zeroth Law 45 min

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Understand temperature and thermal equilibrium.

  • Convert between temperature scales (Celsius, Fahrenheit, Kelvin).
  • Understand thermal expansion and calculate linear and volume expansion.
  • Describe the relationship between temperature and molecular motion.
1

Temperature and Thermal Equilibrium

Temperature determines whether objects are in thermal equilibrium. Two objects in thermal equilibrium have the same temperature. If temperatures differ, energy transfers as heat from hotter to colder.

Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of the particles in a substance. It determines whether energy will transfer between two objects in thermal contact. If two objects have the same temperature, they are in thermal equilibrium, and no net energy transfer occurs.

Temperature is an intensive property—it doesn't depend on the amount of material present. It's fundamentally related to the average kinetic energy of atoms or molecules. Our senses provide a qualitative indication of temperature, but they are unreliable; we need thermometers for quantitative measurement.

2

Thermometers and Temperature Scales

Thermometers measure temperature based on physical properties that change with temperature. Three common scales: Fahrenheit (°F), Celsius (°C), and Kelvin (K).

Thermometers utilize physical properties that change predictably: volume of a liquid, pressure of a gas, electrical resistance, or color of an object.

Celsius: Water freezes at 0°C, boils at 100°C

Fahrenheit: Water freezes at 32°F, boils at 212°F

Kelvin: Absolute scale; 0 K is absolute zero (-273.15°C)

The Kelvin scale is the SI unit. 0 K represents absolute zero, where molecular motion ceases. A temperature change of 1°C equals a change of 1 K. The Fahrenheit degree is smaller (1°F = 5/9 of a Celsius degree). The conversion formulas are:

Celsius to Kelvin TK = TC + 273.15
Celsius to Fahrenheit TF = 1.8 × TC + 32
3

The Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics

If two objects are separately in thermal equilibrium with a third object, then they are in thermal equilibrium with each other. This allows thermometers to measure temperature.

If objects A and B are each in thermal equilibrium with a third object C (a thermometer), then A and B are in thermal equilibrium with each other. This provides a foundation for temperature measurement—a thermometer measures its own temperature, and by the Zeroth Law, that is also the temperature of the object it's in contact with.

The Zeroth Law is called "zeroth" because it was formulated after the First and Second Laws, but it's logically prior to them—it establishes temperature as a measurable quantity. Without it, the concept of temperature would be ill-defined. The law allows us to calibrate thermometers and compare temperatures.

4

Thermal Expansion

Most materials expand when heated and contract when cooled. Linear expansion: ΔL = αL₀ΔT. Volume expansion: ΔV = βV₀ΔT. For solids, β ≈ 3α.

Thermal expansion occurs because particles vibrate more at higher temperatures and move farther apart on average.

Linear Expansion ΔL = αL₀ΔT

where α is the coefficient of linear expansion (°C⁻¹).

Volume Expansion ΔV = βV₀ΔT

where β is the coefficient of volume expansion (°C⁻¹). For solids, β ≈ 3α.

Bridges have expansion joints to accommodate temperature changes. Thermometers rely on the expansion of liquids. Bimetallic strips (two metals with different expansion coefficients) are used in thermostats. Water is unusual—it contracts from 100°C to 4°C, then expands as it cools to 0°C. This is why ice floats and lakes freeze from the top down.

5

Coefficients of Expansion

Different materials expand at different rates. Metals have higher expansion coefficients than glass. Liquids and gases expand much more than solids.

Material Linear Expansion α (°C⁻¹) Volume Expansion β (°C⁻¹)
Aluminum 24 × 10⁻⁶ —
Brass/Bronze 19 × 10⁻⁶ —
Copper 17 × 10⁻⁶ —
Steel 11 × 10⁻⁶ —
Glass (ordinary) 9 × 10⁻⁶ —
Glass (Pyrex) 3.2 × 10⁻⁶ —
Invar (Ni-Fe alloy) 0.9 × 10⁻⁶ —
Mercury — 1.82 × 10⁻⁴
Ethyl alcohol — 1.12 × 10⁻⁴
Gasoline — 9.6 × 10⁻⁴
Air — 3.67 × 10⁻³

Invar has an extremely low coefficient of expansion, useful for precision instruments. Mercury has a relatively high volume expansion coefficient, making it suitable for thermometers. Gases expand much more than liquids or solids for the same temperature change.

🎓 Professor's Narrative

Temperature is one of the most fundamental concepts in physics. It's not just about "hot" and "cold"—it's about the average kinetic energy of the particles in a substance. Thermal equilibrium is the state where no net energy transfer occurs between objects at the same temperature.

The Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics is the foundation of temperature measurement. It's what allows us to say "this thermometer reads 25°C, so the object is also 25°C." Without it, we couldn't calibrate thermometers or compare temperatures.

Thermal expansion is everywhere—from the expansion joints in bridges to the mercury in thermometers. Remember that different materials expand at different rates, which is why bimetallic strips can be used as temperature sensors. And don't forget the exception: water expands when it freezes, which is why ice floats and why pipes burst in winter.

Finally, be careful with temperature conversions. The formulas are simple, but it's easy to make mistakes. Always check your units and remember that the Kelvin scale is absolute—there's no such thing as a negative Kelvin temperature.

❓ Ask the Professor

Absolute zero (0 K or -273.15°C) is the temperature at which molecular motion ceases. According to the Third Law of Thermodynamics, it's impossible to reach absolute zero—you can only approach it asymptotically. As a system approaches absolute zero, the entropy approaches a minimum value, and it takes an infinite number of steps to actually reach 0 K.

When materials are heated, the particles (atoms or molecules) vibrate more vigorously. This increased vibration causes the average distance between particles to increase, which results in expansion. The material expands in all directions, which is why we see both linear and volume expansion.

Water has a unique property due to its molecular structure (hydrogen bonding). In liquid water, molecules are packed relatively closely. When water freezes, the molecules form a crystalline structure (ice) that has more empty space between molecules than the liquid phase. This is why ice is less dense than liquid water and floats.
