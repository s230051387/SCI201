⚡ Direct Current Circuits

EMF, internal resistance, resistors in series and parallel, Kirchhoff's rules, and capacitance

DC Circuits Kirchhoff Capacitance 45 min

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Understand electromotive force (emf) and internal resistance.
  • Analyze circuits with resistors in series and parallel.
  • Calculate equivalent resistance for series and parallel combinations.
  • Apply Kirchhoff's rules to analyze complex circuits.
  • Understand the definition and applications of capacitance.
1

Electromotive Force (emf) and Internal Resistance

The emf (ε) of a battery is the maximum potential difference it can provide. All real batteries have internal resistance (r), which reduces the terminal voltage when current flows: Vterminal = ε - Ir.

A source of emf produces an electric field that causes charges to move around a circuit. The emf ε is the work done per unit charge. All real sources have internal resistance r, which dissipates energy as heat when current flows. The terminal voltage is:

Terminal Voltage Vab = ε - Ir

A perfect battery would have zero internal resistance, but such batteries don't exist. Nickel-cadmium and lead-acid batteries have very low internal resistance. The terminal voltage equals the emf only when no current flows (open circuit). The total power output is P = Iε, delivered partly to the external load (I²R) and partly dissipated internally (I²r).

2

Circuits with Internal Resistance

For a battery with emf ε and internal resistance r connected to a load resistor R, the current is I = ε/(R + r). The terminal voltage is V = ε - Ir = IR.

Applying Kirchhoff's loop rule to a simple circuit with internal resistance:

Loop Equation ε - Ir - IR = 0

Solving for current:

Circuit Current I = ε / (R + r)

If the load resistance is very large (open circuit), current is small and terminal voltage is close to emf. If the load resistance is small (short circuit), current is large and terminal voltage drops significantly. The battery supplies constant emf, but current and terminal voltage depend on the load.

3

Resistors in Series

For resistors in series, the same current flows through each. Equivalent resistance: Req = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + .... Total voltage is the sum of voltage drops.

When resistors are connected end-to-end, current is the same through all: I = I₁ = I₂ = I₃ = ...

Total voltage is the sum of individual voltage drops:

Total Voltage ΔV = V₁ + V₂ + V₃ + ... = I(R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + ...)
Equivalent Resistance Req = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + ...

In a series circuit, if one device fails (open circuit), all devices stop working. This is why household circuits are not wired in series. Series circuits are used where you want the same current through all components, such as in some string lights.

4

Resistors in Parallel

For resistors in parallel, same voltage across each. 1/Req = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃ + .... Req is always less than the smallest individual resistance.

Voltage across each resistor is the same: ΔV = V₁ = V₂ = V₃ = ...

Total current is the sum of branch currents:

Total Current I = I₁ + I₂ + I₃ + ... = V(1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃ + ...)
Equivalent Resistance 1/Req = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃ + ...

In a parallel circuit, each device operates independently. If one fails, others remain on. This is why household circuits are wired in parallel—all devices operate at the same voltage (120 V in the US). Current divides among branches, with more current through paths of lower resistance.

5

Kirchhoff's Rules

Junction Rule: ΣIin = ΣIout (conservation of charge). Loop Rule: ΣΔV = 0 around any closed loop (conservation of energy).

Junction Rule (First Rule): At any junction, the sum of currents entering equals the sum leaving. This follows from conservation of charge.

Loop Rule (Second Rule): When any closed circuit loop is traversed, the algebraic sum of potential changes must equal zero. This follows from conservation of energy.

Loop rule conventions: Resistor in current direction → -IR (drop). Resistor opposite current → +IR. Emf from - to + → +ε. Emf from + to - → -ε. Kirchhoff's rules are essential for circuits with multiple loops and batteries where simple series/parallel reductions aren't possible.

6

Applying Kirchhoff's Rules

1) Label all currents. 2) Apply junction rule for independent current equations. 3) Apply loop rule for voltage equations. 4) Solve the system.

For complex multi-loop circuits, assign a direction to each current (guess; math will correct you if wrong). Apply junction rule to get N-1 independent equations. Apply loop rule to match number of unknowns. Solve the system of linear equations.

If calculation gives a negative current, the actual flow is opposite to your assumption—magnitude is still correct. Kirchhoff's rules always work but require careful sign bookkeeping. Practice is essential for proficiency.

7

Capacitance

Capacitance (C) is the ability to store charge: C = Q/ΔV. SI unit: farad (F). 1 F = 1 C/V. Practical units: μF, nF, pF.

A capacitor stores energy in an electric field between two conductors separated by an insulator. Capacitance is defined as:

Capacitance C = Q / ΔV

For a parallel-plate capacitor in vacuum:

Parallel-Plate Capacitance C = ε₀ A / d

where ε₀ = 8.85 × 10⁻¹² C²/(N·m²).

The farad is enormous. Most capacitors range from microfarad (10⁻⁶ F) to picofarad (10⁻¹² F). Capacitors store energy: U = ½ CV². Applications include filtering, timing circuits, and energy storage.

8

Combinations of Capacitors

Parallel: Ceq = C₁ + C₂ + C₃ + .... Series: 1/Ceq = 1/C₁ + 1/C₂ + 1/C₃ + ....

Parallel: Same voltage across each. Total charge sums: Ceq = C₁ + C₂ + C₃ + ...

Series: Same charge on each. Total voltage sums: 1/Ceq = 1/C₁ + 1/C₂ + 1/C₃ + ...

Capacitors in parallel act like a single capacitor with larger plate area (larger C). Capacitors in series act like a single capacitor with larger plate separation (smaller C). These rules are the reverse of resistors: resistors add in series; capacitors add in parallel.

🎓 Professor's Narrative

Direct current circuits are the foundation of all electrical engineering. The most important concept to master is the relationship between voltage, current, and resistance (Ohm's law). Once you understand this, you can analyze any circuit.

Remember that emf is the source of energy in a circuit, but internal resistance means you can't get all that energy out. The terminal voltage of a battery drops when it's delivering current because some energy is wasted as heat inside the battery.

For combinations of resistors, learn to recognize series and parallel configurations. In series, current is the same through all components, and voltage divides. In parallel, voltage is the same across all components, and current divides.

Kirchhoff's rules are your general-purpose tools for analyzing any circuit. The junction rule is conservation of charge, and the loop rule is conservation of energy. Finally, capacitors store energy in electric fields—their combination rules are the opposite of resistors.

❓ Ask the Professor

The internal resistance causes a voltage drop Ir inside the battery. When you draw more current (smaller load), the internal voltage drop increases, so terminal voltage decreases.

In series, devices share the same current and voltage divides. If one fails, all stop. In parallel, devices share the same voltage and current divides. Each operates independently. Household circuits are parallel.

Adding a parallel resistor provides an additional path for current, reducing overall resistance. Like adding a parallel pipe makes water flow easier.
