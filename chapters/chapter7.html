🔥 Heat and Internal Energy

Heat capacity, specific heat, calorimetry, latent heat, and the First Law of Thermodynamics

Heat Thermodynamics Phase Change 45 min

1

Heat, Internal Energy, and Temperature

Heat (Q) is the transfer of energy across a system boundary due to a temperature difference. Internal energy (U) is the total energy of a system's microscopic components. Temperature measures average kinetic energy of particles.

Heat is the transfer of energy from a hotter object to a colder object due to a temperature difference. It is measured in joules (J) or calories (cal). Internal energy is the sum of all energies associated with microscopic components (translational, rotational, vibrational motion, and intermolecular potential energy). Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of the particles in a system.

It's important to distinguish between heat, internal energy, and temperature. Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of the particles. Internal energy is the total energy of all the particles. Heat is the transfer of energy due to a temperature difference. A large object at a low temperature can have more internal energy than a small object at a high temperature. For example, a bathtub full of warm water has more internal energy than a cup of boiling water, even though the cup has a higher temperature.

2

Heat Capacity and Specific Heat

Heat capacity (C): energy to raise temperature of an object by 1°C: Q = CΔT. Specific heat (c): heat capacity per unit mass: Q = mcΔT. SI unit: J/(kg·°C).

Heat Capacity (C): The amount of energy required to change the temperature of a given sample by 1°C:

Heat Capacity Q = CΔT

C has units of J/K.

Specific Heat (c): The amount of energy required to change the temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1°C:

Specific Heat Q = mcΔT

c has units of J/(kg·°C). Specific heat is a material property. Water has a very high specific heat (4186 J/kg·°C), which is why it takes a long time to heat or cool.

The high specific heat of water is why coastal areas have milder climates than inland areas—the ocean can absorb or release large amounts of heat with small temperature changes. Specific heat varies with temperature, but it's often treated as constant for small temperature ranges. The specific heat of a substance is related to its molecular structure and the degrees of freedom available for energy storage.

3

Calorimetry

Calorimetry is the technique of measuring specific heat by placing a heated sample in water and measuring the final equilibrium temperature. The principle is conservation of energy: heat lost by hot object = heat gained by cold object.

In calorimetry, a sample of known mass is heated to a known temperature, then placed in a calorimeter containing water of known mass and temperature. The system is isolated, so energy is conserved:

Calorimetry Principle Qhot = -Qcold

Since Q = mcΔT, this becomes:

Calorimetry Equation m₁c₁(Tf - Ti₁) = -m₂c₂(Tf - Ti₂)

This equation can be solved for the unknown specific heat.

Calorimetry assumes perfect heat transfer—no heat is lost to the surroundings. In practice, calorimeters are insulated to minimize heat loss. The water equivalent of the calorimeter (including its container) may need to be considered if its mass is significant compared to the water. Calorimetry is used not only to measure specific heat but also to measure latent heats and heats of reaction in chemistry.

4

Latent Heat

Latent heat (L) is the energy absorbed or released during a phase change at constant temperature. The heat of fusion applies to melting/freezing, and the heat of vaporization applies to boiling/condensation. The formula is Q = L·Δm.

During a phase change (such as melting or boiling), energy is absorbed or released without a change in temperature. This energy is used to break or form intermolecular bonds.

Heat of Fusion (Lf): Energy required to change from solid to liquid (melting).

Heat of Vaporization (Lv): Energy required to change from liquid to gas (boiling).

The energy required for a phase change is:

Latent Heat Formula Q = L·Δm

where Δm is the mass that changes phase.

Water has a heat of fusion of 3.33 × 10⁵ J/kg and a heat of vaporization of 2.26 × 10⁶ J/kg. The large heat of vaporization is why evaporating sweat cools us effectively—it takes a lot of energy to turn liquid water into vapor. The energy required to change from solid to liquid (melting) is much less than that required to change from liquid to gas (boiling), because boiling involves completely separating molecules from each other.

5

Phase Changes and Energy Graphs

As energy is added to a substance, its temperature rises, then stays constant during a phase change, then rises again. The slope of the temperature-energy graph during heating is 1/(mc), and the horizontal segments correspond to phase changes.

A typical heating curve for water from ice at -30°C to steam at 120°C shows five distinct segments:

A (Warming Ice): Temperature rises from -30°C to 0°C. Q = miceciceΔT

B (Melting): Temperature stays at 0°C while ice melts. Q = Lfmice

C (Warming Water): Temperature rises from 0°C to 100°C. Q = mwatercwaterΔT

D (Boiling): Temperature stays at 100°C while water boils. Q = Lvmwater

E (Warming Steam): Temperature rises from 100°C to 120°C. Q = msteamcsteamΔT

The horizontal segments in the heating curve represent phase changes, where energy goes into changing the state of matter rather than increasing temperature. The slopes of the sloped segments are determined by the specific heat: a steeper slope means lower specific heat. The total energy required to convert 1 kg of ice at -30°C to steam at 120°C is about 3.1 × 10⁶ J, most of which is required for boiling (vaporization).

6

Supercooling and Superheating

Supercooling is cooling a liquid below its freezing point without it becoming solid. Superheating is heating a liquid above its boiling point without it boiling. Both are metastable states that can be disrupted by nucleation sites.

Supercooling: A liquid can be cooled below its freezing point without solidifying if there are no nucleation sites for crystal formation. Disturbing the liquid causes it to freeze rapidly, releasing the latent heat of fusion.

Superheating: A liquid can be heated above its boiling point without boiling if there are no nucleation sites (like scratches in the container or dissolved gases). Disturbing the liquid can cause explosive boiling, which is why you should be careful when microwaving water in a smooth container.

Supercooling and superheating demonstrate that phase changes require nucleation sites to start. A completely pure liquid can be cooled or heated far beyond its normal transition temperature. The energy released during rapid freezing (after supercooling) can bring the temperature back up to the normal freezing point. This is why water that has been supercooled can freeze almost instantly when disturbed.

7

The First Law of Thermodynamics

The First Law of Thermodynamics is a statement of conservation of energy: ΔU = Q + W. The internal energy of a system increases when heat is added and/or work is done on the system.

The First Law of Thermodynamics states that the change in internal energy of a system is equal to the sum of the heat added to the system and the work done on the system:

First Law of Thermodynamics ΔU = Q + W

where ΔU is the change in internal energy, Q is the heat added to the system (positive if added, negative if removed), and W is the work done on the system (positive if done on system, negative if done by system).

The First Law is a generalization of the principle of conservation of energy to include heat and work. It recognizes that internal energy can be changed by two mechanisms: heat transfer and work. The sign convention varies among textbooks—some use ΔU = Q - W with W as work done by the system. The key is to be consistent with the sign convention. Work done by a gas in a piston is W = -PΔV (when ΔV is positive, the gas does work on the surroundings).

8

Mechanisms of Heat Transfer

Heat can be transferred by three mechanisms: conduction (through direct contact), convection (through fluid motion), and radiation (through electromagnetic waves). Conduction is slow, convection is faster, and radiation is the fastest.

Conduction: Heat transfer through a material by direct contact. Atoms and electrons transfer energy by collisions. Metals are good conductors because of their free electrons. Poor conductors (insulators) include paper, wood, and gases.

Convection: Heat transfer through the bulk motion of a fluid (liquid or gas). Natural convection occurs due to density differences (hotter fluid rises). Forced convection uses a fan or pump.

Radiation: Heat transfer through electromagnetic waves (infrared radiation). Radiation does not require a medium and can occur through a vacuum. All objects emit thermal radiation, with the rate proportional to T⁴ (Stefan-Boltzmann law).

The three mechanisms often act simultaneously in real systems. For example, a hot radiator heats a room primarily by convection and radiation, but also by conduction through its surface. The rate of heat conduction through a material is given by Fourier's law: P = kA(ΔT)/L, where k is thermal conductivity. The rate of radiation is given by the Stefan-Boltzmann law: P = εσAT⁴, where ε is emissivity.

🎓 Professor's Narrative

Heat and internal energy are fundamental concepts in thermodynamics. Remember that heat is not a substance—it's energy in transit. When you add heat to a system, you're transferring energy to it, which can increase its internal energy or cause it to do work.

The distinction between temperature, heat, and internal energy is crucial. Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy per particle. Internal energy is the total energy of all particles in the system. Heat is the energy transferred due to a temperature difference.

Specific heat is a material property that tells you how much energy is required to change the temperature of a given mass. Water's high specific heat is why it's used as a coolant in engines and why coastal climates are mild.

Phase changes are fascinating because they involve energy transfer without temperature change. Latent heat is the energy required to overcome intermolecular forces. The large latent heat of vaporization of water is why sweating is such an effective cooling mechanism.

The First Law of Thermodynamics is the foundation of all energy analysis in thermal systems. It tells us that energy is conserved in all processes, whether through heat transfer or work.

❓ Ask the Professor

Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of the particles in a system. Heat is the transfer of energy due to a temperature difference. A small amount of boiling water has a high temperature but less total heat energy than a large amount of warm water. Heat is energy in transit; temperature is a property of a system.

When sweat evaporates (changes from liquid to vapor), it absorbs latent heat of vaporization from your skin. This removes energy from your body, lowering your temperature. The cooling effect depends on the humidity—in humid conditions, the air is already saturated with water vapor, so evaporation is slower and cooling is less effective.

Boiling requires nucleation sites—places where bubbles can form (like scratches on a container or dissolved gases). If a liquid is very pure and the container is smooth, there are no nucleation sites, so the liquid can be heated above its boiling point without boiling. Disturbing the liquid provides nucleation sites, causing explosive boiling.
