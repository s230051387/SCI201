⚡ Current and Resistance

Electric current, Ohm's law, resistance, resistivity, and electrical power

Current Resistance Ohm's Law 45 min

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Define electric current and understand its direction.
  • Understand current density and its relationship to drift velocity.
  • Apply Ohm's law and understand the concept of resistance.
  • Calculate resistance from resistivity and geometry.
  • Understand how resistance varies with temperature.
  • Calculate electric power and energy in circuits.
1

Electric Current

Electric current is the rate of flow of charge through a region of space. The SI unit is the ampere (A), where 1 A = 1 C/s. Current direction is defined as the direction positive charges would flow.

Electric current (I) is the rate at which charge flows through a surface. If ΔQ is the amount of charge that passes through area A in time Δt, then the average current is:

Average Current Iavg = ΔQ / Δt

The direction of current is the direction in which positive charges flow when free to do so. In ordinary conductors, this is opposite to the direction of electron flow.

The ampere is one of the seven base SI units. Current is a macroscopic quantity that arises from the microscopic motion of charge carriers. In metals, the charge carriers are electrons, but the convention of positive current flow dates back to Benjamin Franklin and is still used today. In electrolyte solutions, both positive and negative ions can carry charge.

2

Current Density

Current density (J) is the current per unit area: J = I/A. For a conductor with charge carriers of density n, charge q, and drift velocity vd, J = n q vd.

Current density is a vector quantity that describes how current flows through a cross-sectional area. If the current density is uniform and A is perpendicular to the current direction:

Current Density J = I / A

The SI units of J are A/m². In terms of microscopic quantities:

Microscopic Form J = n q vd

Drift velocity is the average velocity of charge carriers in the presence of an electric field. It is typically very small (on the order of mm/s), yet currents can be large because there are an enormous number of charge carriers. For example, in a copper wire with a current of 1 A, the drift velocity of electrons is only about 0.1 mm/s.

3

Ohm's Law

Ohm's law states that the current density J in a conductor is proportional to the electric field E: J = σE, where σ is the conductivity. Materials that obey this are called ohmic.

For many materials (especially metals), the ratio of current density to electric field is a constant σ (the conductivity):

Ohm's Law (Field Form) J = σE

Equivalently, in circuit terms: V = IR. Materials that obey Ohm's law are said to be ohmic. Materials that do not are called non-ohmic. Ohm's law is an empirical relationship, not a fundamental law of nature.

The resistance R of a conductor determines the current for a given voltage. The microscopic origin of resistance is collisions between charge carriers and the atoms of the conductor. These collisions transfer energy from the charge carriers to the lattice, increasing the temperature of the conductor. This is why wires heat up when current flows through them.

4

Resistance and Resistivity

Resistance (R) is the ratio of voltage to current: R = V/I. Resistivity (ρ) is a material property related to resistance by R = ρL/A, where L is length and A is cross-sectional area. The SI unit of resistance is the ohm (Ω).

Resistance is a measure of how difficult it is for current to flow through a conductor. It arises due to collisions between electrons and fixed atoms. The resistance of a uniform conductor of length L and cross-sectional area A is:

Resistance Formula R = ρ L / A

The SI unit of resistance is the ohm (Ω), where 1 Ω = 1 V/A. The SI unit of resistivity is Ω·m.

Resistance depends on both the material (through ρ) and the geometry (through L and A). This is why long, thin wires have more resistance than short, thick wires. A good conductor has low resistivity, while a good insulator has very high resistivity (often 10¹⁰ to 10¹⁶ Ω·m).

5

Temperature Dependence of Resistance

Resistance typically increases with temperature for metals. The change is approximately linear: R = R₀[1 + α(T - T₀)], where α is the temperature coefficient of resistance.

Over a limited temperature range, the resistivity of a metal varies approximately linearly with temperature:

Temperature Dependence ρ = ρ₀[1 + α(T - T₀)]

Since resistance is proportional to resistivity:

Resistance Change R = R₀[1 + α(T - T₀)]

The temperature coefficient α is positive for metals (resistance increases with temperature) because increased thermal vibrations cause more collisions. For semiconductors, α is negative (resistance decreases with temperature) because more charge carriers become available. At very low temperatures, some materials become superconductors with zero resistance.

6

Electrical Energy and Power

Electric power is the rate at which energy is transferred: P = IV. For a resistor: P = I²R = V²/R. The SI unit is the watt (W).

The power (rate of energy transfer) is:

Electrical Power P = IΔV

For a resistor, using Ohm's law (ΔV = IR), the power dissipated as heat is:

Power in Resistor P = I²R = (ΔV)² / R

The energy dissipated in a resistor over time t is E = Pt = I²Rt. This is why resistors get hot. The power delivered by a battery to a circuit is P = εI, where ε is the emf. Part goes to the load, part is dissipated internally. This is the Joule heating effect.

7

8

Power in Circuit Elements

The power delivered to a resistor appears as internal energy (heat). For a battery, total power output is P = Iε, and part is dissipated internally.

For a circuit with battery emf ε and internal resistance r connected to external resistor R, the current is I = ε/(R + r). The power delivered to the external load is:

Load Power PR = I²R

The total power delivered by the battery is:

Total Power P = Iε = I²R + I²r

Maximum power is delivered to the load when R = r (maximum power transfer theorem). In practical circuits, we minimize internal resistance so most power goes to the load rather than being wasted as heat inside the battery.

🎓 Professor's Narrative

Current and resistance form the foundation of all electrical circuits. Remember that current is the flow of charge, and resistance is what opposes that flow. The relationship between voltage, current, and resistance is elegantly captured by Ohm's law: V = IR. However, remember that Ohm's law is not a fundamental law—it's an empirical observation that holds true for many materials but not all.

Pay special attention to the distinction between resistance (a property of a specific object) and resistivity (a property of a material). A long, thin wire has more resistance than a short, thick wire made of the same material because resistance depends on geometry.

The temperature dependence of resistance is crucial in real-world applications. Metals increase in resistance when heated, which is why light bulbs have a lower resistance when cold. Semiconductors decrease in resistance when heated, which makes them useful as temperature sensors.

Finally, always remember that power is the rate of energy transfer. When you see a resistor in a circuit, it's converting electrical energy into heat. This Joule heating is both useful (in heaters, toasters) and wasteful (in power transmission lines).
