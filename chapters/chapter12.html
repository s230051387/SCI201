💡 The Nature of Light, Reflection and Refraction (Part 1)

History of light, electromagnetic waves, reflection, and Snell's law

Light Reflection Refraction 45 min

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Understand the nature of light as an electromagnetic wave.
  • Apply the law of reflection.
  • Apply Snell's law of refraction.
  • Understand the concept of index of refraction.
  • Calculate the speed of light in different media.
1

The Nature of Light

Light is an electromagnetic wave that can propagate through vacuum. It exhibits both wave and particle properties. The wave nature explains interference and diffraction; the particle nature explains the photoelectric effect.

Historically, light was considered a stream of particles (Newton's corpuscular theory). In 1678, Huygens showed that a wave model could explain reflection and refraction. In 1801, Young demonstrated interference, providing clear evidence for the wave nature of light. In 1873, Maxwell showed that light is an electromagnetic wave. In the early 20th century, experiments showed that light also has a particle nature—the particles are called photons.

Light is both a wave and a particle (wave-particle duality). The wave nature is observed in interference and diffraction experiments. The particle nature is observed in the photoelectric effect and Compton scattering. The speed of light in vacuum is c = 3.00 × 10⁸ m/s.

2

Ray Propagation and the Ray Approximation

In the ray approximation (geometric optics), light travels in straight lines in a uniform medium. This approximation is valid when the wavelength of light is much smaller than the size of the objects or openings it encounters.

Ray optics (or geometric optics) assumes that light travels in straight lines called rays. This approximation is valid when the wavelength of light is much smaller than the size of the objects or openings it encounters. If the opening is comparable to the wavelength, diffraction occurs, and the ray approximation breaks down.

The ray approximation is very useful for understanding reflection, refraction, mirrors, and lenses. It assumes that light travels in a fixed direction in a straight line through a uniform medium and changes direction when it meets the surface of a different medium. This is a good approximation for many everyday situations, including vision, photography, and optical instruments.

3

Reflection

The law of reflection states that the incident ray, the reflected ray, and the normal to the surface all lie in the same plane, and the angle of reflection equals the angle of incidence: θr = θi.

When a light ray strikes a smooth surface (like a mirror), it reflects according to the law of reflection:

1. The incident ray, the reflected ray, and the normal to the surface are all in the same plane.

2. The angle of reflection equals the angle of incidence: θr = θi.

Specular reflection occurs from a smooth surface. Diffuse reflection occurs from a rough surface.

The law of reflection is a consequence of the wave nature of light—it's the same as the reflection of any wave. It's used in mirrors, periscopes, and many optical instruments. The angle is always measured from the normal (perpendicular) to the surface, not from the surface itself.

4

Refraction and Snell's Law

Refraction is the bending of light when it passes from one medium to another. Snell's law relates the angles of incidence and refraction: n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂.

When light passes from one transparent medium to another, part of it reflects and part of it transmits (refracts). The refracted ray bends according to Snell's law:

Snell's Law n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂

where n₁ and n₂ are the indices of refraction, θ₁ is the angle of incidence, and θ₂ is the angle of refraction. When light enters a medium with higher index of refraction (n₂ > n₁), it bends toward the normal (θ₂ < θ₁).

Snell's law is derived from the wave nature of light—it's a consequence of the wavelength changing when light enters a different medium. The index of refraction is defined as n = c/v, where c is the speed of light in vacuum and v is the speed of light in the medium. All materials have n ≥ 1.

5

Index of Refraction

The index of refraction of a medium is n = c/v, where c is the speed of light in vacuum and v is the speed of light in the medium. Higher index means light travels slower in the medium.

The index of refraction n is a measure of how much light slows down in a material:

Index of Refraction n = c/v

All materials have n ≥ 1. The index depends on the wavelength of light (dispersion), which is why a prism separates white light into its colors. Typical values: Air: 1.0003, Water: 1.33, Glass (crown): 1.52, Glass (flint): 1.66, Diamond: 2.42.

The index of refraction is a material property that depends on the atomic structure. In general, denser materials have higher indices. The speed of light is slower in materials because light interacts with atoms—it's absorbed and re-emitted, causing a delay. The index also depends on wavelength, which is why different colors are bent by different amounts (dispersion).

🎓 Professor's Narrative

Light is a fascinating phenomenon—it's both a wave and a particle. For most everyday optics (reflection, refraction, mirrors, and lenses), the wave nature is sufficient, and we can use the ray approximation.

Reflection is straightforward—the angle of reflection equals the angle of incidence. This simple law governs how mirrors work, from bathroom mirrors to telescopes.

Refraction is the bending of light when it enters a different medium. Snell's law n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂ is the key to understanding refraction. When light enters a medium with a higher index (slower speed), it bends toward the normal. When it enters a medium with a lower index, it bends away from the normal.

The index of refraction n = c/v is a measure of how much light slows down in a material. Diamond has a very high index (2.42) because light travels very slowly in it. This high index is responsible for diamond's brilliance.

❓ Ask the Professor

Light interacts with the atoms in the material—it is absorbed and re-emitted by the atoms, which causes a delay in the propagation. The more atoms the light interacts with (denser material), the more it slows down.

Light from the pencil travels from water (higher index) to air (lower index), so it bends away from the normal. This makes the pencil appear to be bent or broken at the water's surface. This is a classic example of refraction.

Diamonds have a very high index of refraction (2.42), which means light bends a lot when it enters and leaves the diamond. The critical angle for diamond is small, so light that enters the diamond undergoes total internal reflection many times before it exits, creating the characteristic sparkle.
